Day-by-Day Itinerary

1

Rogersville, Tennessee
Drive from South Mississippi to one of Tennessee's oldest and least-known historic towns. Population 4,400. Settled in 1775 by Davy Crockett's grandparents. About 475 miles, 7-8 hours driving (plan 9+ with breaks).
Depart South Mississippi
Route: I-59 N to Birmingham ‚Üí I-59 N to Chattanooga ‚Üí I-75 N to Knoxville ‚Üí I-81 N to Exit 8 (Rogersville). The last hour is beautiful Appalachian foothills on a quiet interstate.
Break Stop: Birmingham area
About 2-2.5 hours from home. I-59/20 Exit 133 for the famous Irondale Cafe ‚Äî the original 'Whistle Stop Cafe.'
Break Stop: Chattanooga
About 4.5 hours from home. Good lunch stop. Avoid downtown congestion by using I-24 bypass or stopping at exits north of town.
Arrive Rogersville
Check into Hale Springs Inn ‚Äî built 1824, hosted Presidents Andrew Jackson, James K. Polk, and Andrew Johnson. Nine rooms with period furnishings but modern amenities. The oldest continuously operating inn in Tennessee (with a brief closure 1999-2003). Ground floor rooms available.
Dinner at McKinney's Tavern
On-site at Hale Springs Inn. Named for the original builder. Southern cuisine in a historic setting. Reservations recommended.
2

Rogersville / Norris, Tennessee
Morning exploring Tennessee's second-oldest town, afternoon at the crown jewel of Appalachian museums. About 60 miles between sites.
Downtown Rogersville walking tour
Self-guided walk through downtown. See the Hawkins County Courthouse (1836, oldest still in use in TN), Masonic Temple (oldest lodge in TN, 1805), Tennessee Newspaper and Printing Museum (site of state's first newspaper, 1791). Pick up walking tour map at Hale Springs Inn.
Crockett Spring Park
Where Davy Crockett's grandparents settled in 1775. Rogers Cemetery has graves of town founder Joseph Rogers and the Crocketts. Peaceful park with the famous Ebbing and Flowing Spring ‚Äî one of only two tidal springs in the world.
Drive to Norris via back roads
Route: US-11W south through Bulls Gap ‚Üí TN-32 to Morristown ‚Üí US-25E/11W to Norris. About 1.5 hours. Scenic route through small communities ‚Äî Bean Station, Morristown outskirts. Alternatively, take I-81/I-40 (faster but less scenic).
Museum of Appalachia
65-acre Smithsonian Affiliate with 35+ authentic log cabins, 250,000+ artifacts, working farm with heritage breed animals. One of the best folk life museums in America. Founded by John Rice Irwin who spent decades collecting buildings and artifacts from throughout the region.
More Info ‚Üí
Dinner options in Clinton/Norris area
The Vols Diner in tiny Norris village has classic American comfort food. Harrison's Grill & Bar in Clinton has good food and outdoor seating.
3

Pall Mall, Tennessee
Visit the homestead of WWI's most decorated soldier in the remote Wolf River valley. Pall Mall population: 200. This is REAL back roads Tennessee. About 75 miles from Clinton, 1.5-2 hours.
Drive to Pall Mall
Route: I-75 N to Caryville ‚Üí US-25W to Oneida ‚Üí TN-297 to Jamestown ‚Üí US-127 N to Pall Mall. The last stretch on US-127 follows the Wolf River through a beautiful isolated valley. Gas up in Jamestown ‚Äî no services in Pall Mall.
Sgt. Alvin C. York State Historic Park
Alvin York was one of the most decorated soldiers of WWI, capturing 132 German prisoners in a single action. Born poor in this valley, he returned here after the war and lived the rest of his life farming and working to improve education in the area. Park includes his two-story home (guided tours available), the grist mill he operated on the Wolf River, his general store, a WWI trench replica, and the York Bible Institute he founded.
More Info ‚Üí
Wolf River Methodist Church
Just outside the park. Where York had his religious conversion and where he and Gracie were married by the Governor of Tennessee in 1919. Simple white frame church, very moving.
Pickett CCC Memorial State Park (nearby)
If time permits, this remote park 15 miles south has beautiful CCC-era stone structures and is one of the least-visited state parks in Tennessee. Natural bridge and rock formations.
Drive to Crossville for overnight
About 1 hour south on US-127. Crossville is a small city (pop. 12,000) but has good lodging options and is positioned well for tomorrow's drive.
4

Spruce Pine, North Carolina
Scenic drive through the mountains to the tiny 'Mineral City' of North Carolina. Population 2,100. About 175 miles, 4-5 hours with winding mountain roads.
Route to Spruce Pine
I-40 E to Asheville bypass (I-240) ‚Üí I-26 E toward Erwin, TN ‚Üí US-19W through Erwin ‚Üí NC-197/US-19E through remote communities to Spruce Pine. The stretch through Erwin and up into NC is beautiful and very lightly traveled.
Break Stop: Erwin, Tennessee
Small railroad town in the Nolichucky River valley. Good leg stretch. Home to the Nolichucky Gorge ‚Äî one of the deepest gorges in the Eastern US.
Arrive Spruce Pine & Explore Downtown
Spruce Pine has two 'main streets' ‚Äî Upper Street (Oak Avenue) and Lower Street (Locust Avenue). Walk both. Stop at Toe River Arts Council Gallery for local art. Cross the Footbridge over the North Toe River to Riverside Park.
Check in at Richmond Inn
Bed & breakfast in a 1939 home. Or try the Switzerland Inn at Little Switzerland (15 minutes away on the Blue Ridge Parkway) for mountain views.
Dinner: Tropical Grill or DT's Blue Ridge Java
Tropical Grill has Caribbean-influenced cuisine (unusual for this area!). DT's is good for lighter fare ‚Äî sandwiches, coffee, ice cream.
5

Penland / Little Switzerland, NC
Visit America's preeminent craft school in its remote mountain setting, then explore a tiny resort village. Penland is 6 miles from Spruce Pine. Little Switzerland population: 46.
Penland School of Craft
Internationally renowned craft school founded 1929, sprawling across 420 acres of Blue Ridge terrain. Visitors welcome at the Gallery and Visitors Center (exceptional contemporary craft for sale), the Craft House (one of NC's largest log buildings), the Supply Store, and the resident artist studios at The Barns where working artists often have open doors.
More Info ‚Üí
Little Switzerland
Tiny resort village right on the Blue Ridge Parkway (Milepost 334). Population 46. Named for its Swiss Alps-like views. The Switzerland Inn has been here since 1910. Browse the General Store, have coffee at Little Switzerland Books & Beans (multiple rooms stuffed with used books), and admire the mountain views.
Museum of North Carolina Minerals (Free)
At Blue Ridge Parkway Milepost 331. NPS museum with interactive displays on 300+ minerals and gems from the Spruce Pine Mining District. Learn why this area has some of the purest quartz on Earth (used in computer chips!).
More Info ‚Üí
Blue Ridge Parkway driving
Drive a section of the Parkway between attractions. Milepost 331-339 has beautiful overlooks and connects to Crabtree Falls if you want an easy waterfall hike.
Dinner at Switzerland Inn or Chalet Restaurant
The Switzerland Inn has mountain views from the dining room. Chalet Restaurant also in Little Switzerland.
6

Crossnore / Burnsville, NC
Visit a historic weaving room that's been operating since 1920, then explore an arts-focused small town. Crossnore population: tiny (unincorporated). Burnsville population: 1,700.
Crossnore Weavers and Gallery
Working museum in a National Historic Site building. Watch weavers at their looms creating traditional Appalachian patterns ‚Äî some have been weaving here for 30+ years. The weaving program started in 1920 to teach mountain women a marketable skill. Upstairs gallery features local pottery and art. Proceeds support Crossnore Communities for Children.
More Info ‚Üí
E.H. Sloop Chapel & Ben Long Fresco
Just across the road from the Weaving Room. Small chapel with a Ben Long fresco 'Suffer the Little Children' (completed 2006). Ben Long is internationally known for his frescoes throughout the NC mountains.
Drive to Burnsville
About 20 miles via US-19E. Pretty drive through mountain valleys.
Downtown Burnsville
Charming small town in Yancey County, surrounded by the Black Mountains (including Mount Mitchell). The Town Square has towering trees and is the heart of an active arts community. Browse galleries and studios ‚Äî many local artists. The town hosts the Mount Mitchell Crafts Fair each August.
Dinner in Burnsville
Several good local restaurants around the square. Garden Deli has fresh sandwiches and salads. Fresh Wood Fired Pizza is popular.
7

Brasstown, North Carolina
Drive to America's oldest folk school. Brasstown population: tiny (unincorporated). About 90 miles from Burnsville, 2-2.5 hours through mountain roads.
Drive to Brasstown
US-19W through Burnsville ‚Üí US-19 south through remote mountain communities ‚Üí Brasstown. This route goes through Mars Hill, Weaverville outskirts, and then climbs into increasingly rural areas. OR take US-19E to Asheville and then US-19/74 west (faster, less scenic).
John C. Campbell Folk School
Oldest folk school in America (founded 1925). 270-acre campus with 17 studios for blacksmithing, weaving, woodworking, basket making, broom making, and more. Visitors welcome to observe classes and watch artisans at work. The Craft Shop is a founding member of the Southern Highland Craft Guild ‚Äî excellent for authentic handmade items. History Center tells the story of the school's Danish-inspired origins.
More Info ‚Üí
The Crown Brasstown
RIGHT NEXT TO the Folk School, overlooking a scenic valley. True farm-to-table using Brasstown beef and local ingredients. Entirely gluten-free menu. The best restaurant for many miles ‚Äî 'world class dining where you feel like you're at the neighborhood pub.' Reservations recommended.
