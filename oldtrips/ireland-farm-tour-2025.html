IRELAND PRACTICAL INFORMATION

Emergency Contacts

Emergency Services: 112 or 999 (Police, Fire, Ambulance)
U.S. Embassy in Dublin: +353 1 668 8777

Currency & Payments

  • Currency: Euro (€)
  • Credit Cards: Widely accepted; Visa and Mastercard most common
  • Tipping: Not as extensive as in the US
    • Restaurants: 10-15% for good service (check if service charge already included)
    • Taxis: Round up to nearest euro or 10% for longer journeys
    • Tour guides: €5-10 per person for a good tour
    • Hotel porters: €1-2 per bag
    • Bartenders: Not typically tipped for drinks

Irish Driving Tips

  • Drive on the LEFT side of the road
  • Roundabouts are common (traffic moves clockwise)
  • Rural roads can be very narrow with stone walls and limited passing spaces
  • Speed limits are in kilometers:
    • Motorways (M roads): 120 km/h (75 mph)
    • National roads (N roads): 100 km/h (62 mph)
    • Regional roads (R roads): 80 km/h (50 mph)
    • Urban areas: 50 km/h (31 mph)
  • Your rental car will likely be manual transmission (unless specifically requested otherwise)
  • Fuel is called "petrol" (gasoline) or "diesel" - confirm which your car requires
  • Familiarize yourself with rental car insurance coverage before departure

Weather & What to Expect

November in Ireland:

  • Temperature range: 40-50°F (4-10°C)
  • Frequent rain showers; can be windy
  • Daylight hours: approximately 8am-4:30pm (short days)
  • Occasional morning fog, especially in rural areas

Local Customs & Etiquette

  • Greetings: Irish people are friendly and informal. A simple handshake and "hello" is fine.
  • Pub Etiquette:
    • Order and pay at the bar (table service is rare except in restaurants)
    • Round buying is common in groups (each person takes turns buying drinks for everyone)
    • "Cheers" or "Sláinte" (pronounced "slawn-cha") is the toast
  • Conversation: Irish people value wit and storytelling. Politics and religion are generally avoided with strangers.
  • Phrases:
    • "Grand" = fine/good
    • "Craic" (pronounced "crack") = fun/entertainment/news
    • "What's the craic?" = How are you?/What's happening?

Mobile & Internet

  • Free WiFi is available in most accommodations and many cafés/pubs
  • Cell coverage is good in towns and cities but can be spotty in remote areas
  • Your US phone will work with international roaming activated
  • Ireland uses Type G electrical outlets (UK-style three-pin plugs) - bring adapters

IRISH MUSIC GUIDE

Your itinerary includes nightly traditional music experiences. Here's what to know:

Types of Sessions

  • Scheduled performances: Musicians are paid to perform
  • Open sessions: Local musicians gather to play together informally
  • Singing sessions: Focus on unaccompanied traditional singing

Etiquette

  • Keep conversation low during performances
  • It's fine to clap after tunes, but not during them
  • If you know a song and are invited to join, keep it traditional
  • Asking musicians to play "Danny Boy" is considered touristy

Instruments to Watch For

  • Fiddle, flute, tin whistle, uilleann pipes (Irish bagpipes)
  • Bodhrán (Irish drum), accordion, banjo, mandolin, harp

Venues on Your Itinerary

  • The Cobblestone (Dublin): Authentic traditional sessions
  • Darkey Kelly's (Dublin): Lively performances
  • The Corner House (Cork): Intimate sessions
